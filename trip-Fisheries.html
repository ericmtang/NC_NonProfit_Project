Voyage- Student

Indonesia

Sustainable Fisheries Development

Dive into the lush landscapes, inviting waters, and rich traditions of the Hawaiian Islands this summer, from Kona to Waimea to Hilo. These volcanic islands have evolved a rich and unique biology and culture, both of which are under pressure from outside forces, ranging from tourism to climate change and invasive species. On this high school service program, collaborate on projects centered on environmental conservation and cultural revitalization as you work with local organizations, meet with Native Hawaiians, and learn about the islands from a unique perspective. Itinerary

This itinerary represents our best projection of the group’s schedule. However, we may implement changes designed to improve the quality of the program. Note that the June 29th and July 15th departures follow a slightly modified itinerary. Please call for details.

Meet your fellow high school student travelers and one or more of your leaders in Los Angeles, and fly together to Kona, Hawaii.

Begin your program by exploring the wild volcanic landscapes of the Kona coast. Participate in an in-depth orientation to get to know your group, then meet with our service partners and begin to learn about Hawaiian culture. Jump into the rich, vibrant waters offshore and lay the groundwork for an understanding of Hawaii’s many diverse ecosystems.

Head to Waimea, the Big Island’s cowboy country, and partner with Ulu Mau Puanui, an organization dedicated to understanding and preserving ancient Hawaiian agricultural practices and to educating students about sustainability. Take part in beach cleanups, collect scientific data to monitor the health of the Kohala watershed, help plant native crops, and educate visitors on the conservation of native coral and fish species. Spend a day with the Kailapa Community Association learning about traditional Hawaiian communities and assisting with their outreach programs. In the afternoons, trek to rope swings hidden in the forest or take a surfing lesson and catch a wave at a nearby surf break.

Cap your exploration of the Big Island by traveling to the wild eastern side, where countless waterfalls and deep rainforest await, contrasting the semi-arid environment of the west. Stop at Mauna Kea, a dormant volcano known as White Mountain for its snow-covered peak, rising over two miles above the ocean, and explore Volcanoes National Park along the way. Join service projects in protected areas to help restore critical habitat and native species that have lost ground to industrial farming and invasive species. Learn how ancient Hawaiians navigated by the stars, visit the world-famous Hilo Farmers’ Market, visit incredible beaches and hidden forest sites, and try your hand at outrigger canoeing and other Hawaiian cultural practices.

Fly from Kona, Hawaii, to Los Angeles with your group and a leader, then continue on to your final destination.

A Day in the Life: Waimea

Due to the traveling nature of this high school service program, each day is different. Here is a snapshot of a day in Waimea.

8am Breakfast with your group in the cafeteria at your lodgings at a local school

9am Start your service work planting traditional crops at the Puanui garden site

12pm Break for lunch on a grassy hill overlooking the ocean

1pm Head down the road to the Pololu Valley Lookout and hike to a secluded beach

5pm Trek back up to the road and get dinner at the organic cafe in Hawi

8pm Group meeting to discuss the day’s work and learning

What to Expect

During your time in Hawaii, you can expect to work on many different volunteer projects—for instance, removing plastic trash from remote beaches, planting native crops, and working with researchers to learn about shark, turtle, and monk seal rehabilitation projects. The focus of this program is learning about another culture and ecosystem by forming meaningful relationships with local people and undertaking shared service experiences together. You should come to this summer program with an open-mind, eager to participate in new experiences and interested in exploring another culture and way of life. Everyone participates in all of the group’s projects.

Pursue an independent project and explore an aspect of local culture of particular interest to you—Hawaii’s culture, geology, or conservation efforts, for example. You will have designated project time to create a final project to share your learning with your peers. Formats can range from a formal presentation to an interactive workshop to a photography portfolio, and your group leaders can help you develop a topic and carry out the project.

This is a physically active summer travel program. The group will go on long hikes and engage in moderately strenuous service activities. You do not need to be at peak fitness to participate, but it is important that you have a desire to be physically active, and that you are excited about trying all activities.

This program visits a few high altitude destinations—Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa both rise above 13,000 feet, and we will take short visits above 8,000 feet in elevation.

At our community service sites, we stay in simple but comfortable dormitory-style accommodations. Sleeping areas and bathrooms are divided by gender. In Kona and Hilo, we stay in hostels, in single-gender rooms with 2-4 beds. Leaders reside with students throughout the program.

We make and pack our own lunches most days, especially on service days at remote locations. We enjoy breakfast and dinner from small Hawaiian establishments, so the group has a chance to taste the incredible fusion flavors of the islands.
Voyage Indonesia

Destination

Hawaii is the birthplace of surfing, hula, and the ukulele, among countless other contributions to global culture. The most remote island chain on Earth, these islands were first settled by Polynesian explorers 1500 years ago; since then, many waves of immigration have followed. The islands were annexed by the U.S. in 1898, against the will of Native Hawaiians, who proudly maintain their cultural traditions and language to this day. From coral reefs and fresh lava flows to snowcapped volcanic peaks and the wettest place in the U.S., the landscape offers incredible variety and opportunity for exploration. >

Language

English and Hawaiian are both official languages of Hawaii; many of our service partners and contacts are Native Hawaiians who are fluent in both. You will have an opportunity to learn phrases and practice the Hawaiian language along the way!

Climate

Hawaii is home to the majority of the climatic zones found on Earth, from tropical rainforests to alpine tundra. Expect hot days with temperatures above 80°F, and warm nights in the 60s. At high elevations the temperatures will dip into the 50s. Prepare for some windy days.

Cuisine

Hawaiian food is rooted in a fusion of ancient crops and techniques brought by the earliest Polynesian settlers with the foodways of the Portuguese, American, Chinese, Japanese, and Korean cultures (among many others!) that followed. Expect to try local staples like taro and passionfruit alongside modern dishes like poke and spam musubi.

Next Steps

This program is directed by Lauren Zimmerman. If you have questions or would like to talk further about this program, please get in touch!

Lauren Zimmerman

Marine Biology Instructor

Lauren has her Masters in Marine Studies and has participated in research at multiple universities around the country to study the impacts of climate change on marine ecosystems. Her interests are focused on ocean acidification and environmental justice for coastal communities.
